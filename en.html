January 7th (Wednesday)
I realized that I was repeating the "feeling unwell due to the cold after the New Year" pattern again this year. (If I had taken sufficient measures at that time, I wouldn't have quit my job last year, I could have held on.) As a physical measure, I decided to buy a large insulation board from a home improvement store. During the summer after I quit my job, I had applied light-blocking and heat-insulating film to my windows. In the summer, it was good because it improved the effectiveness of the air conditioner and reduced the glare from outside, but I felt many disadvantages from autumn and winter onwards. It was dark even during the day, and at night it was just as cold as without the film. I had bought the frosted glass type, so when I wanted to watch meteor showers or fireworks displays at night, I had to open the window and let in the cold air. I chose film because I didn't want it to look cheap, but I should have just bought an insulation board from the beginning. I had been avoiding double-glazed windows or replacing the windows because I felt it would mean living at my parents' house for a long time. In terms of communication, I reaffirmed that the first action should be "don't stay silent when you're feeling unwell, and specifically state what kind of ailment you're experiencing." When I say I'm "not feeling well," it's mostly "90% fatigue + 10% impaired judgment," but if I stay silent, I just look like "a silent person who's putting on a show and demanding special treatment," so it's better to speak up. More often than not, the other person will tell me something similar, and we can find a solution. Yet, when I'm feeling unwell, I worry about things like "What if they talk back when I say I'm not feeling well?" or "What if it makes everyone around me gloomy?" It's obvious that ignoring a gloomy face will worsen the atmosphere at home and at work. There are training institutions that teach health management before you start working, and support organizations that provide care while you work, but from my experience using them, I think they're insufficient. When I was feeling unwell, I wasn't honest, or my self-perception was too distorted, and there were many times when I rejected better care that I could have received. On that note, I think it would be great if there was something like a "mental health RPG." Reading various survivors' accounts is good, but I think it would be nice to have an experience where you can rapidly iterate through situations where you roll back to just before a game over, and even further back, and choose different actions to improve your prognosis, thereby learning how to cope in the real world. You can't experience that level of rapid iteration in actual communities or training institutions. I'd also like a "Project Management RPG."
